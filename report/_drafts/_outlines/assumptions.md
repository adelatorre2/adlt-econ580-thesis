# Assumptions

**RQ:** To what extent did PDUFA disproportionately impact the share of controlled substances FDA approved each year?

**Hypothesis:** PDUFA allowed firms to pay for faster review → firms prioritized their most profitable drugs → the most profitable drugs tend to be those with inelastic demand (i.e., addictive substances) → controlled substances grew as a share of FDA approvals post-PDUFA.

---

## Demand-side

**A1.** Controlled substances face inelastic demand from patients — they serve essential medical needs (e.g., ADHD, pain management) and patients have few substitutes.

**A2.** They also face inelastic demand in illicit/diversion markets — addiction creates price-insensitive demand.

**A3.** Inelastic demand → higher profitability — firms can sustain wider margins and more predictable revenue on products where buyers are less price-sensitive.

## Firm behavior

**A4.** Firms are profit-maximizing — R&D and regulatory resources flow toward highest expected return, not highest public health benefit.

**A5.** Government incentives (orphan drug credits, priority review vouchers, public funding) don't substantially redirect firm behavior away from profit-maximizing choices.

**A6.** Firms actively prioritize which drugs to push through the pipeline — they don't submit everything at once; they choose.

## PDUFA mechanism

**A7.** PDUFA's fee-for-speed structure lets firms selectively accelerate their most profitable drugs — paying fees effectively buys faster market entry.

**A8.** Speed to market is more valuable for high-margin drugs — the return to earlier approval is higher for controlled substances, so the mechanism disproportionately benefits them.

**A9.** The same logic applies to generic manufacturers (ANDA filers) — faster generic entry for controlled substances is especially valuable.

## Institutional context

**A10.** DEA scheduling typically follows FDA approval, not precedes it — firms may not know at submission time whether a drug will become scheduled, so scheduling isn't a deliberate target.

**A11.** Medicare/Medicaid reliably covers controlled substances — public payer coverage backstops manufacturer revenue expectations.

**A12.** Less-diversified firms feel this pressure most acutely — the profit-maximizing prioritization logic is strongest for firms that can't spread risk across a large portfolio.

---

**Composite:** PDUFA → firms selectively accelerate profitable drugs → controlled substances are disproportionately profitable → share of controlled substances among approvals rises post-PDUFA.
