\section{Introduction}

Drug overdose deaths reached 107,000 in the United States in 2021, the highest annual total on record, and prescription controlled substances have played a prominent role throughout the overdose epidemic \parencite{markDrugOverdoseDeaths2024}. % TODO: verify citation — exact 2021 overdose figure
Understanding how regulatory institutions shape the legal supply of controlled substances is therefore a question with first-order policy consequences.

The FDA is the primary gatekeeper of the U.S. pharmaceutical market. Its decisions determine which drugs may be sold, how quickly they reach patients, and---through the review process---what evidence of safety and efficacy is required before approval \parencite{carpenterINTRODUCTIONGatekeeper2010}. A large literature examines the speed-safety tradeoff at the FDA: faster review times accelerate patient access to beneficial new treatments, but deadline pressure may also reduce the scrutiny applied to drugs with later safety problems \parencite{carpenterDrugreviewDeadlinesSafety2008, philipsonCostbenefitAnalysisFDA2008}. The Prescription Drug User Fee Act (PDUFA) of 1992 stands at the center of this literature: by authorizing the FDA to collect manufacturer fees in exchange for binding review-time targets, PDUFA substantially cut median approval times and changed the incentive environment facing both the agency and the firms it regulates \parencite{berndtIndustryFundingFDA2005, olsonPDUFAInitialUS2009}.

A parallel literature studies the economics of controlled substances. Pharmaceutical opioids, stimulants, benzodiazepines, and other DEA-scheduled drugs are clinically important but carry non-trivial abuse potential. Supply-side interventions in these markets can have unintended consequences: \textcite{alpertSupplySideDrugPolicy2018a} show that abuse-deterrent OxyContin reformulation reduced prescription misuse but shifted users toward heroin, illustrating how the composition of the legal drug supply feeds upstream into illicit-market behavior. % TODO: verify citation

What no study has examined is whether PDUFA---or its generic-drug analogue, the Generic Drug User Fee Amendments (GDUFA) of 2012---changed the composition of FDA approvals in ways that matter for controlled-substance supply. If review-time incentives disproportionately benefit high-margin drug classes, and if controlled substances systematically generate higher margins through patent-protected branded formulations, then regulatory acceleration may have altered the mix of approved drugs even without any deliberate policy intent.

This paper provides the first systematic test of that hypothesis. Using the complete Drugs@FDA database (1939--2025, $N = 25{,}908$ original approvals) linked to DEA scheduling records, I estimate interrupted time series and difference-in-differences specifications around both PDUFA (1992) and GDUFA (2012). The central finding is a null result with an important qualification. The aggregate controlled-substance share of FDA approvals fell after PDUFA, from 9.6\% pre-1992 to 7.3\% post-1992. A Poisson rate model restricted to new drug applications---where the PDUFA incentive is most direct---yields an incidence-rate ratio of 1.46 that is marginally significant ($p = 0.06$) under year fixed effects but collapses to 1.19 ($p = 0.62$) once group-specific time trends are added. A drug-level investigation of the 2009--2013 CS-NDA spike that drives the apparent effect reveals it to be an industry opioid product cycle (OxyContin reformulation, Exalgo, Embeda, Suboxone, and related extended-release and abuse-deterrent formulations), not a PDUFA composition effect. For GDUFA, the ANDA controlled-substance share declined significantly in the post-performance-goal era (IRR $= 0.67$, $p < 0.001$), which is inconsistent with the regulatory acceleration hypothesis for generic drugs.

The broader implication is that the speed-safety tradeoff literature should be extended to downstream market-composition questions with care: the controlled-substance composition of FDA approvals is not stable over time, but the variation appears to reflect industry product cycles rather than regulatory incentives.
