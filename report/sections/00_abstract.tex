\begin{abstract}

The Prescription Drug User Fee Act (PDUFA) of 1992 authorized the FDA to collect industry user fees in exchange for performance targets on review times. While a large literature documents that PDUFA substantially accelerated drug approval, no study has asked whether regulatory acceleration also changed the \textit{composition} of approved drugs---specifically, whether the share of approvals classified as controlled substances under the Controlled Substances Act changed as a result. Using the Drugs@FDA database linked to DEA scheduling records (1939--2025, $N = 25{,}908$ original approvals), I estimate interrupted time series and difference-in-differences specifications around both PDUFA (1992) and the Generic Drug User Fee Amendments (GDUFA, 2012). The overall controlled-substance share of FDA approvals fell after PDUFA. Focusing on new drug applications (NDAs), where the policy incentive is most direct, a Poisson rate model yields an incidence-rate ratio of 1.46 ($p = 0.06$) that is not robust to group-specific trend controls. A late-2000s spike in Schedule~II NDA approvals (OxyContin reformulation, Exalgo, Embeda, and related products) accounts for the apparent post-PDUFA rate increase. For GDUFA, the ANDA controlled-substance share fell significantly post-reform ($\hat{\beta} = -0.40$, IRR $= 0.67$, $p < 0.001$). The evidence does not support the hypothesis that user-fee-funded regulatory acceleration systematically increased the controlled-substance share of FDA approvals.

\end{abstract}
