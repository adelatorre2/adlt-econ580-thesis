\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper asks whether PDUFA (1992) and GDUFA (2012)---the two major user-fee-funded regulatory acceleration reforms at the FDA---changed the controlled-substance share of original drug approvals. Using the complete Drugs@FDA database (1939--2025, $N = 25{,}908$) linked to DEA scheduling records, I estimate interrupted time series and stacked difference-in-differences specifications for both the NDA pathway (PDUFA) and the ANDA pathway (GDUFA).

The central finding is an empirical null for PDUFA and a direction-reversing result for GDUFA. The aggregate CS share of FDA approvals fell after 1992. The NDA-only Poisson rate model yields an IRR of 1.46 that is marginally significant under year fixed effects but not robust to group-specific trend controls. A drug-level investigation confirms that the apparent post-PDUFA elevation in CS NDA approvals is driven by a specific 2009--2013 industry product cycle---extended-release and abuse-deterrent branded opioid formulations---rather than by a sustained PDUFA-induced composition shift. For GDUFA, the ANDA CS share fell significantly in the post-performance-goal era (IRR $= 0.67$, $p < 0.001$), inconsistent with the hypothesis that faster generic review disproportionately benefited CS products.

The findings suggest that the standard economic argument for why user-fee-funded regulatory acceleration should raise the CS share of approvals---that CS drugs have higher expected margins and therefore more to gain from faster review---does not find support in the approval data. The controlled-substance composition of FDA approvals appears to be driven by industry product cycles and application-mix trends rather than by review-time incentives at the regulatory margin. This does not imply that FDA regulatory policy is irrelevant to controlled-substance supply: approval decisions are a necessary condition for market entry, and any drug on the list in Table~\ref{tab:spike_drugs} required FDA clearance before it could be prescribed. But the compositional variation is better explained by demand for specific formulations in the pharmaceutical product cycle than by PDUFA incentives.

For researchers and policymakers, the implication is that the causal pathway from regulatory acceleration to controlled-substance supply likely runs primarily through the innovation investment channel---firms deciding whether to develop CS products---rather than through the review-time channel. Understanding that investment decision, and how factors like patent term, market exclusivity, and prescribing-market demand conditions shape it, may be more productive for policy design than focusing on FDA review-time performance goals. Future work linking the upstream approval-side evidence here to downstream prescribing volumes and ultimately to health outcomes would provide a more complete picture of how pharmaceutical regulation shapes controlled-substance supply across the full market chain.
